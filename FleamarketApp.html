User Interface Design of Fleamarket App
(April 2022)


Overview

  • Project type: Part of a university coursework
    My role: UI & Visual Design (Logo, Concept Realisation)
    Deliverables: New Interface, Log In Page, Live Prototype
    Tools: Adobe XD, Illustrator

Story

The world's natural resources are finite and reducing the demand for them is becoming an important part to fight against the overuse of these raw materials. It is not just at an industrial level that this fight occurs. Reducing the general demand for products is another way to help. Many that still can be of some use end up filling the landfills. Recycling these unwanted items is becoming necessary.


Facts

• Traditionally, charity shops in the UK were able to reuse and recycle more than 90% of donated books and 85% of electrical goods.
• Councils around the UK have to pay around £100 per tonnes for waste going to these landfills.
• Reducing the use of landfill reduces the CO2 footprint.
• In 2018/2019, 339'000 tonnes of textiles where kept out of landfill just by charity retail.
• Charity shops provide items such as clothes at an affordable price.


Challenge

Design an interface for an online mobile app called FleaMarket that will enable adults to sell and buy second-hand unwanted items such as household items, equipment, tools and belongings etc.


Approach

Starting off by focusing on the "Welcome Page" and "Log in/Sign up Pages", first impressions are key. By easing in the access to the app, the goal is to make the user feel familiar and comfortable, ready to purchase or sell an item and help the environment. Since this project is technically tasked at a later stage in a design process, I am working with a pre-determined material. After I was given access to data and a pre-made interface to improve upon, my task was clear.

Simplify navigation, streamline and add visual elements that stay consistent with branding.

Despite not being a stated requirement, I believe a logo and consistent elements of visual design related to branding is integral. Consistency is key to make an interface intuitive.

Screens

I then played around with the idea of adding an informative fullscreen carousel as an intro to the app (part of the Welcome pages). However, I am limited on resources to conduct proper user testing on wether it is practical or even needed. Therefore, working through some of my regular approach is to research competitors (in this case Vinted, Depop and Poshmark), as well as sketch some new visual elements.

User Flow

User opens the app for the first time.

The app displays a welcome screen as its loading the log in page.

User is met with a toggled choice for either the "Log in" (default) or "Sign up" option.

User can choose to log in with other social media accounts.


User enters their username and password and taps the "Log in" button.

The app verifies the user's credentials and logs them in.


User chooses sign up and enters a new username and password.

The app verifies the user's credentials and shows a pop-up displaying a message for email confirmation.

The app verifies the user's credentials and logs them in.

The app navigates to the feed page, which displays a list of items posted from other users.

User can scroll left or right through the feed to view posts.

User can access a page for all items via menu hamburger button.

User can access their own profile page from the feed page by tapping their profile picture or username.

User can log out of the app from the profile icon by tapping the "Log out" button on the Profile page.
